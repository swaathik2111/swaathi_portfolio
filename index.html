SkillMatch AI
AI-Powered Job Matching Platform · Mobile App · Fully Coded · HTML / CSS / Vanilla JS
AI Product DesignMobile App Fully Coded4 Weeks Solo Project
The Problem & Goal
The Problem
Job seekers struggle to understand exactly why they are rejected. Existing platforms match by keyword — not by actual skill depth. Users don't know which skills are missing, how critical each gap is, or where to learn them. Platforms treat a Beginner and an Expert as identical to the algorithm.
The Goal
  • Match users to jobs by weighted skill scoring
  • Show exactly which skills are missing and why
  • Provide a direct learning path via YouTube courses
  • Track completion and show improved match score in real time
  • Support 7 career roles with role-specific skill profiles

Key Design Decisions
Weighted AI Match Score

Inline Learning Path
Curated YouTube courses mapped per skill gap — visible directly inside each job detail screen. Course completion triggers live score recalculation with a green border glow, showing progress instantly.
Luxury Design System
Cormorant Garamond (display) + Plus Jakarta Sans (body). 5-layer surface depth scale. Full dark/light CSS variable token system. Glassmorphism floating nav. Zero external UI libraries.
Technical Build
Single-file HTML/CSS/Vanilla JS app. Animated SVG match rings. localStorage session persistence. Mobile-first 430px layout. 13 real job listings across 7 career roles. 20+ curated learning courses. Firebase-ready service layer.

Portfolio 02 · UX Case Study · 2026
NurtureNest
Premium Baby Care Mobile App · iOS & Android
Mobile UXHealth & Wellness Fully Coded4 Weeks3 Rounds Usability Testing
The Problem & Goal
The Problem
Parents of newborns face extreme sleep deprivation and decision fatigue. Existing apps compound the stress — cluttered interfaces, too many taps, no caregiver sharing, and clinical alerts that make parents feel judged rather than supported.
The Goal
  • ≤ 2-tap logging from any screen, any time
  • Pass the "3am one-handed test" on every screen
  • Reduce parental anxiety through calm, warm design language
  • Shared caregiver access for grandparents and babysitters
  • Intelligent insights — reassurance, not alarms
Core Pain Points
  • Cognitive overload at 3am — cluttered UI, too many taps, unusable one-handed
  • Emotionally cold design — clinical alerts make parents feel judged, not supported
  • Fragmented tracking — 3–4 apps for sleep, feeds, diapers, growth
  • No caregiver sync — grandparents abandon apps within 3 minutes due to complexity
Tools Used
Figma · FigJam · Maze · Notion · HTML / CSS / JavaScript

Fully functional HTML/CSS/Vanilla JS prototype — real state management, localStorage persistence, live timers, animated SVG charts, spring physics easing. Zero dependencies.

Key Design Decisions
Quick-Log FAB
Floating Action Button accessible from every screen. Any activity — feed, sleep, nappy, growth — logged in ≤ 2 taps. Background sleep timer persists even when app is closed. Optimised for dark conditions, one-handed use.
Caregiver Mode
Three oversized buttons, 18px minimum text, simplified navigation, large tap targets. Grandparents and babysitters can log confidently without needing a tutorial. Designed after Kamala persona failed 3 competitor apps in under 3 minutes.
Warm Design Language
All notification copy rewritten from clinical ("REMINDER: FEED BABY") to warm conversational tone ("Emma usually feeds around now 🍼"). Auto dark mode activates 9pm–7am. WHO percentiles shown in plain language, not alarming charts.
Warm Midnight Design System
Dark-mode-first colour palette. Iterated from original light pastel system after Round 1 testing found it unusable at 3am. 3 full rounds of design iteration across 18 participants before reaching 91 SUS score.

  • Live employee search, filter, sort & pagination
  • AI insight engine with rule-based anomaly detection

Portfolio 04 · UX Case Study · Social Impact Design
AgriConnect
Farm-to-Consumer Platform · Web UX · Connecting Smallholder Farmers Directly to Urban Consumers
Web UXSocial Impact MultilingualOffline-FirstFigma
The Problem & Mission
The Problem
Smallholder farmers in rural India earn only 20–30% of the retail produce price through middlemen, while urban consumers pay a premium for non-fresh food. No accessible digital bridge exists — compounded by low bandwidth, multilingual needs, and minimal UX literacy in rural areas.
The Mission
Eliminate middlemen by connecting farmers directly to consumers — giving farmers 70%+ of market price and consumers fresher produce at fair cost. Three hard design constraints: low bandwidth / offline-first, Tamil + English multilingual, and feature-phone to smartphone transition UX literacy.

Research Findings
Key Insights
  • Farmers own feature-phones but are transitioning to basic Android via family members
  • Rural users rely on visual cues (images, icons) far more than text labels for navigation
  • Urban consumers want freshness guarantee and source transparency — willing to pay same price
  • WhatsApp is the primary digital channel for both personas — familiar interaction patterns informed design
Design Constraints
  • Low bandwidth: App must work on 2G/3G rural networks. Offline-first with sync indicators.
  • Multilingual: Tamil and English toggle throughout. Iconography over text for low-literacy users.
  • Minimal UX literacy: Voice input, image-heavy UI, simple 2-step flows. Feature-phone habits honoured.
User Personas
Ravi — The Farmer
38 · Smallholder Farmer · Salem, Tamil Nadu · 2 acres · Primary Persona
"I need a simple, visual app to list my produce and connect with city buyers — without middlemen giving me only 20–30% of market price. I trust voice over text and check my phone morning and evening."
Priya — The Urban Consumer
31 · Software Engineer · Chennai · ₹70,000/month · Secondary Persona
"I want to find and subscribe to trusted local farms — supermarket produce is expensive, over-processed, and I can't verify the source. I'm willing to pay ₹20–30 more per kg for transparency."
Key Design Decisions
Farmer Dashboard
Image-heavy listing UI so farmers can photograph crops to list them. Voice input for pricing (no typing). Tamil / English toggle throughout. Offline-first with sync status bar. Visual quantity cards (Bag / Crate / Bundle) replacing per-kg tables for low-literacy users. Simple order notifications in Tamil.
Consumer Marketplace
Farm discovery map with freshness indicators and harvest date badges. Subscription basket for weekly orders from favourite farms. Transparent delivery pricing — no hidden fees. Filter by organic, region, and farm rating. Direct farm contact for repeat orders.
Accessibility
WCAG AA contrast throughout. 44px minimum tap targets for motor difficulty. All key actions reachable in ≤ 3 taps. Iconography used in place of text labels wherever literacy constraints apply. Tested against 2G loading constraints.
Deliverables
User Research Report · 2 Detailed Personas · User Journey Maps · Information Architecture · Low-Fi Wireframes · High-Fi Figma Prototype · Accessibility Audit · Design Challenge Finalist presentation.

Portfolio 05 · UX Case Study · 3 Weeks
QuickBite
Mobile Food Ordering App · Transparent Pricing · UPI-First · Multi-Shop Ordering
Mobile UXConsumer App 3 Weeks2 Rounds Usability TestingFigma Prototype
The Problem & Goal
The Problem
Users struggle to find affordable, quick food delivery with transparent pricing. Current platforms show total price only at checkout — causing cart abandonment. Local food vendors avoid platforms due to high commission rates (25–30%), limiting consumer choice. Cluttered interfaces cause decision fatigue, especially for first-time users.
The Goal
Design an intuitive food delivery app that prioritises:
  • Affordability — clear pricing upfront, no hidden costs
  • Speed — 15–20 minute ordering, 5-minute app flow
  • Transparency — vendor info, delivery time, full cart breakdown
  • Multi-shop ordering — up to 3 shops, single delivery
  • UPI-first payment for the Indian market
User Research
10+
User Interviews
20+
Online Surveys
4

Test Participants
4 Core Pain Points
  • High commissions — 25–30% reduces vendor participation
  • Hidden costs — price revealed only at checkout causes cart abandonment
  • Generic local experience — no regional food culture, no neighbourhood vendors
  • Complex interfaces — high cognitive load, can't add items from multiple shops
Competitive Audit
Zomato · Swiggy · Uber Eats · Snackpass

None showed transparent pricing at browse stage. None supported multi-shop ordering in one cart. None were optimised for UPI-first payment flow as default. All had cluttered home screens with excessive filters.
User Personas
Busy Bharti
28 · Software Engineer · Madipakkam · ₹50k–70k/month · 45% of users
"I need lunch in 10–15 minutes. Hidden fees at checkout make me late for meetings and blow my ₹150 budget."
Budget Bhushan
22 · College Student · Chennai Central · ₹10k–15k/month · 35% of users
"Surprise charges make me feel cheated. I can't compare prices across restaurants and minimum order limits stop me buying small."
Health Hema
35 · Fitness Enthusiast · Siruseri · ₹60k–80k/month · 20% of users
"I research extensively before ordering but vendors misrepresent dishes as healthy and I can't verify ingredients."
Key Design Decisions
Transparent Pricing from Home
Price ranges (₹50–150) displayed on restaurant cards before the user even taps in. No more checkout surprises. Users can filter by budget from the home screen — a feature zero competitors offered.
Cart Breakdown
Line-by-line breakdown: Food Price + Delivery Fee + Tax = Total. No hidden costs, ever. Before/after usability testing showed cart abandonment dropped to zero after this change was implemented.
Multi-Shop Ordering
Users can add items from up to 3 different shops in one cart and receive everything in a single delivery. Unique feature not available on Zomato, Swiggy, or Uber Eats — directly solving the "I want chai from one place and snacks from another" use case.
UPI-First Payment
100% of Round 2 test users preferred UPI (PhonePe, Google Pay) over cards. Cards moved to secondary option. Real-time ETA with live tracking replaced anxiety-inducing delivery ranges ("30–45 mins"). 44px minimum tap targets throughout.
Usability Testing — Before & After
Round 1 — Lo-Fi (5 users)
  • 3/5 completed ordering flow without getting lost
  • 2 users confused by back navigation — used browser back
  • Restaurant comparison was requested by 2 users
  • ETA ranges caused anxiety — "just tell me the exact time"
Round 2 — Hi-Fi (8 users)
  • All users completed full ordering flow
  • Location persistence resolved address confusion
  • UPI set as default — cards moved to secondary
  • Live ETA in minutes resolved delivery anxiety
